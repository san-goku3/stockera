Frequently Asked Questions about IPO

Find answer to common questions that come in your mind related to IPO.


How to Subscribe to an IPO? +

  1. Login to your respective service provider.
  2. Click on the IPO button.
  3. Select the IPO you want to bid and enter the relevant details.
  4. Your subscription will be completed once you make the payment or give permission.

Should I buy an IPO on the first day? +

It depends on various factors such as the demand, market conditions, and the company's potential. It's advisable to do thorough research or consult with a financial advisor before making a decision.

How do you know if an IPO is good? +

Research the company's financials, growth potential, industry trends, and market conditions. Reading the prospectus and analyzing expert opinions can also help in assessing an IPO's potential.

How to check IPO start date? +

You can check the IPO start date on the stock exchange's website, financial news portals, or through your service provider's platform.

What is issue size? +

The issue size refers to the total amount of funds a company aims to raise through the IPO. It is calculated by multiplying the total number of shares offered by the price per share.

How many shares in a lot? +

The number of shares in a lot varies depending on the company's decision and the regulatory guidelines. It is specified in the IPO prospectus.

How is the lot size calculated? +

The lot size is determined by the company and the stock exchange. It ensures that retail investors can buy shares in manageable quantities.

Who decides the IPO price band? +

The IPO price band is decided by the company's management in consultation with investment bankers and underwriters, based on the company's valuation and market conditions.

What is IPO GMP? +

IPO GMP (Grey Market Premium) refers to the premium at which shares are traded in the grey market before they are officially listed on the stock exchange.

How many lots should I apply for in an IPO? +

The number of lots to apply for depends on your investment capacity and risk appetite. Applying for more lots increases your chances of allotment, but also requires a higher investment.
